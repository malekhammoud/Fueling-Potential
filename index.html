Fueling Potential

A 3D-Printed Exploration of Energy Poverty and Human Ambition.

The Story Behind the Light

"Fueling Potential" is a 3D-designed, 3D-printed lithophane sculpture shaped like a traditional jerry can. By day, it appears as a stark, industrial vessel. But when illuminated from within, it reveals two deeply personal images: a vulnerable newborn baby in a hospital, and a young student studying late into the night.

These images are my own. I was born in Lebanon, a country where the power grid is fragile and reliable electricity is never guaranteed, and I now live and study in Canada. The contrast in my life reflects the stark reality of global energy poverty, which means a lack of reliable access to modern energy sources. Energy poverty isn't just an inconvenience; a lack of reliable energy supply can stall school attendance, hospital operations, and communication. The baby in the incubator symbolizes how access to energy dictates healthcare and survival. The student represents how electricity in schools and homes means longer study hours and improved learning.

I chose the jerry can to house these lithophanes to force the viewer to confront an uncomfortable truth: addressing energy poverty requires physical infrastructure and traditional fuels. Today, over 80% of global energy still comes from fossil fuels. Even delivering sustainable solutions, such as solar panels to off-grid communities, requires a significant amount of fossil energy for manufacturing and transportation. Fossil fuels are foundational for producing the steel, cement, and infrastructure needed to build a cleaner world. My sculpture argues for a blended energy future, integrating renewables and fossil fuels, as the most realistic solution facing humanity today.

This is where the Canadian energy sector becomes crucial. In energy-rich countries, we often take energy for granted. Yet, Canadian oil and gas are produced under world-leading environmental standards, making them among the most ethical and responsible energy sources. Furthermore, Canada is abundant in Liquefied Natural Gas (LNG) resources, which boast one of the lowest greenhouse gas footprints among fossil fuels. This unique combination gives Canada a distinct global advantage in helping to end energy poverty.

However, my sculpture's "fuel" is trapped, much like Canada's energy potential. Currently, Canada's limited export capacity is a massive missed opportunity for global impact. A lack of pipelines prevents Canada from exporting to global markets, stalling production, deterring investment, and costing us our global influence.

Through technical precision, utilizing Fusion 360 and 3D printing, I built "Fueling Potential" to physically manifest the connection between raw natural resources and human ambition. It serves as a reminder that access to energy is essential for breaking the cycle of poverty. Canada has the resources and the responsibility to do more; we just need the infrastructure to let our light reach the rest of the world.
